Overview

Here's the thing about Zoom: it was built for meeting rooms — screen sharing, scheduling, thirty minutes and out. Nobody designed it for a classroom. But in 2020, when schools shut down overnight, it became one anyway, simply because it was the tool already sitting on everyone's laptop. That was five years ago. Hybrid learning stopped being an emergency and turned into just... how school works now. And somewhere in that shift, AI became the thing every EdTech company is racing to get right.

So I asked a fairly blunt question: if you rebuilt Zoom from scratch as an actual classroom tool — not a meeting app with a few education features bolted on — what would that even look like? This case study is the answer, worked through across four milestones: figuring out where the market opportunity actually sits, going and asking teachers what's broken, making real design decisions about layout and AI behaviour, and finally documenting a prototype that holds together as a system.

Problem Statement

"K–12 teachers are using Zoom as a classroom every day — but Zoom was designed for boardrooms."

That line is basically the whole project in one sentence. Meanwhile 88% of students now use generative AI for their coursework, which means the bar for what a classroom tool needs to do just moved, fast. Teachers are stuck in the middle: no attendance tracking, no way to see who's actually engaged, nothing for academic integrity, no real LMS connection — and every competitor around them is closing that gap faster than Zoom is.

Why K–12 teachers, specifically
🎯
The selection is driven by adoption leverage, market timing, AI anxiety as an entry point, and Google Classroom's growing feature advantage — which Zoom can counter with superior conferencing.
  • Market size — ~3.8M K–12 public school teachers in the US; ~84M teachers globally. The US K–12 EdTech market is projected to grow from $18B (2024) to $55.4B by 2030 at a 20.6% CAGR.
  • Highest platform frequency — K–12 teachers run sessions 5 days a week, 180+ days a year, the highest usage frequency of any segment, translating to high switching cost and retention value.
  • AI anxiety is an onboarding opportunity — 60% already use AI, but 85% feel unprepared. Zoom's no-extra-cost AI Companion can be the platform that makes AI feel safe and guided rather than threatening.
  • Google Classroom's conferencing weakness — the leading K–12 LMS, serving ~60,000 US schools, is rated lower than Zoom on video quality and reliability. Teachers already use both tools; Zoom can consolidate the workflow.
  • Bottom-up institutional influence — district technology decisions are heavily shaped by teacher adoption and feedback.
  • Business impact — $15/teacher/month across 3.8M US K–12 teachers is up to $684M ARR at full penetration; even 5% share is ~$34M ARR with a path to $200M+ over three years.

Synthesis

Key insights

Six things kept surfacing once I put the research side by side. Some confirmed what I expected going in; a couple genuinely surprised me.

01

Zoom is already in the classroom — but AI changes the stakes

Zoom has near-universal presence in education by meeting usage. In 2026 the question is no longer whether a platform has video — it's whether it has the right AI layer. AI Companion 3.0 puts Zoom meaningfully in the race; the remaining gap is education-specific packaging and structural LMS workflow features that AI alone can't replace.

02

The AI readiness gap is the #1 unmet need

85% of teachers use AI, but ~71% still lack formal training. This isn't a technology gap — it's a confidence and integration gap. The platform that scaffolds AI into existing workflows, without requiring teachers to become AI experts, wins K–12 adoption.

03

Student AI adoption has hit critical mass — institutions are scrambling

With 88% of students using generative AI for academic work, every education platform now needs an AI governance and academic-integrity story. Zoom doesn't have one yet — both a risk and an opportunity.

04

Blackboard's bankruptcy opens a migration window

Institutions on Blackboard — roughly 12% of North American higher-ed institutions by count — are in active re-evaluation. Zoom has a narrow window to position as part of the modern alternative stack, particularly through deeper Canvas integration.

05

The LMS market grew beyond its own forecasts

The global LMS market reached ~$28.58B in 2025, ahead of earlier projections, on a confirmed path to $70.83B by 2030. Growth is now driven as much by AI integration demand as by basic e-learning adoption.

06

Asia-Pacific is the next frontier

With Asia-Pacific projected as the fastest-growing region in both LMS and AI-in-education markets, the next EdTech growth wave is international — and Zoom's global infrastructure and multilingual AI Companion are an asset here.

Sources — Systems Thinking & Mapping Outcomes (Milestone 1), §8.
Opportunity Areas

Five product directions, three business bets

Not every opportunity here is worth chasing at once. I've ranked the three strongest business cases at the bottom — the rest are worth having on the roadmap.

1 · Zoom Classroom Mode — K–12 purpose-built interface

Real-time auto attendance with LMS export, per-student engagement indicators, AI-generated lesson recaps, and a teacher-facing lesson plan view.

Why now: teachers already use Zoom and AI Companion — but in a generic business UI. A purpose-built classroom interface would eliminate 2–3 external tools and directly address workflow fragmentation, the #1 pain point.

2 · Zoom AI Academic Integrity Layer

AI contribution detection in submissions, an institutional policy builder, a student AI audit trail, and an academic-integrity dashboard.

Why now: 88% of students use generative AI for coursework, and no major conferencing platform has an academic-integrity AI product yet. This white space grows every semester.

3 · Zoom + Canvas / LMS deep integration (higher ed)

Auto-synced attendance to the Canvas gradebook, auto-archived recordings, AI lecture summaries delivered into Canvas, and LTI 1.3 compliance.

Why now: Canvas now holds greater US higher-ed LMS share than its next three competitors combined, and Blackboard's bankruptcy creates a migration window.

4 · Zoom AI for student personalisation

Personalised study guides from session engagement, an AI tutoring bot trained on course content, and at-risk student flagging for instructors.

Why now: no dominant player offers AI-adaptive learning inside a video-conferencing platform, and students already use external AI for this — Zoom can bring it inside where institutions can monitor it.

5 · Zoom for Parents — K–12 communication layer

AI-generated class highlights and attendance notifications, in-app parent-teacher scheduling, and weekly AI-summarised progress reports.

Why now: Google Classroom's guardian feature is widely used but basic and text-only — a richer, AI-native parent experience would directly challenge Google Classroom's primary K–12 stickiness feature.

Primary Research

What teachers actually said

Market reports are useful, but they don't tell you what it actually feels like to run a class through Zoom on a Tuesday morning. So I ran a short survey and sat down with one teacher directly, just to sanity-check everything above against a real voice.

Survey observations

A few things stood out from the responses. Video-conferencing tools eat up more than half of these teachers' working time — no surprise there. What did stand out was how much Google Meet and Microsoft Teams show up alongside Zoom, which says people are cobbling together whatever integrates best rather than committing to one platform. Opinions on AI tools were genuinely split, not uniformly enthusiastic or skeptical. Nobody was happy with how their LMS talks to their video tool. And when I asked what frustrates them most during or after a live class, and what they'd actually want built in, the answers were specific enough to design against. The full survey form and the raw response sheet are both linked if you want to look at everything directly.

💬
One open-ended response described a live session where a connectivity failure dropped several students and forced a mid-lesson restart. Another respondent said they use neither AI tools nor Zoom at all, preferring custom software. A third had no complaints to report.
One-on-one interview

The one interview I ran went deeper on something the survey only hinted at: licensing. This teacher's school juggles multiple software subscriptions that each renew separately, and it's a real administrative headache. Their ask was simple — one platform, one subscription, one less thing to manage. They also wanted a tablet-and-pen feature built directly into the LMS, which wasn't something I'd anticipated going in.

Foundation
01 · Structure

Component-driven design system

A library of reusable primitives — cards, buttons, dialogs, sheets, tooltips, progress bars, charts, sidebars — organised under a centralised /ui directory.

Consistent button, card, and interaction behaviour across every screen reduces the teacher's learning curve and builds interface trust over repeated use.
08 · Layout

Desktop-first educational workspace

A persistent sidebar, multi-column dashboard grids, and simultaneous visibility of class data, AI recommendations, and student metrics — collapsing gracefully on mobile.

Teachers manage attendance, engagement, and communication simultaneously; the 8-item sidebar means zero clicks to reach any section from any view.
09 · Accessibility

Educational SaaS accessibility

High-contrast text (#111827 on #FFFFFF, exceeding WCAG AA), 18px card padding, 32px section gutters, and a clear type hierarchy from 20px headings to 13px metadata.

Teachers can scan a page's structure in under two seconds; consistent spacing reduces mis-clicks during fast-paced live sessions.
The AI Layer
02 · AI experience

AI-first classroom assistance

The AI assistant is a conversational workspace, not a passive notification feed — it surfaces context-aware recommendations, generates quizzes and recaps, and drafts follow-up emails in real time.

Teachers get actionable recommendations — pacing alerts, engagement summaries, at-risk flags — right after class, without navigating to a separate analytics view.
03 · Interaction model

Conversational interaction model

Chat-based interaction: user/assistant message separation, typing indicators, timestamps, and prompt shortcuts.

One panel handles quiz generation, recaps, pacing advice, and parent-email drafting — no separate view needed per task.
07 · Motion

Motion & micro-interaction design

Subtle, purposeful motion via Framer Motion — animated message entry, hover transitions, expand/collapse states — constrained to avoid overwhelming users.

Typing indicators prevent uncertainty during AI processing; short animation durations preserve a calm SaaS aesthetic appropriate for a classroom tool.
Making Data Actionable
04 · Visualization

Semantic educational data visualization

Purpose-built charts — progress bars, engagement trend lines, attendance bars, risk indicators — each optimised for rapid interpretation over comprehensive exploration.

Summary stat cards (Total Students, High Risk, Avg Engagement, Avg Score) give the full class picture before any scroll.
05 · Risk model

Student risk classification system

Students are auto-categorised into High / Medium / Low risk tiers from a composite of attendance, engagement, and academic performance — colour-coded and filterable.

A teacher managing 70+ students across three classes can identify the students needing immediate outreach in a single filtered view, without manual analysis.
06 · Disclosure

Progressive disclosure UX

Default views show only essential data; expandable rows, collapsible panels, and hover-triggered detail cards reveal depth only on request.

The AI suggestion panel collapses to a badge count and expands into full recommendation cards on demand, keeping the primary view scannable.
11 · Control

Data filtering & teacher control

Text search, multi-key sorting, risk-level filter chips, and AI accept/dismiss actions across the analytics and attendance views — all stateful.

Filtering to "High Risk" reduces a 12-student list to the 2 that need attention immediately; a name search finds any student in under 3 seconds.
Brand & Context
10 · Aesthetic

Zoom-aligned professional aesthetic

Neutral surfaces, minimal shadows, semantic blue accents, and rounded cards that deliberately avoid futuristic AI visual tropes — mirroring Zoom's own product language.

Teachers already familiar with Zoom recognise the navigation patterns instantly, lowering the perceived barrier to adopting a new product.
12 · Context

K–12 educational context integration

Every feature is framed through an educator's lens — class recaps, parent communication, attendance risk, lesson pacing — rather than a generic meeting participant's lens.

Navigation reflects teacher tasks — Dashboard → Classes → Students → Attendance → AI → LMS → Reports — instead of Zoom's default meeting-host structure.

Next Steps

Future enhancements from usability feedback

Usability testing turned up two ideas I hadn't designed for — both good enough that they're now first in line for the next iteration.

Finding 01

Personalised AI recommendations per student

User suggestion: provide AI-based suggestions for each student or guardian, based on that student's individual performance data — not just class-level recommendations.

Proposed feature — Student-Level AI Insight Cards:

  • Each student row expands to an AI-generated insight panel alongside the existing trend chart
  • Insights are generated from composite data: attendance rate, engagement trend, quiz scores, participation history
  • Three output types — a teacher-facing note (e.g. "Jamie's engagement has dropped 18% over 3 weeks — consider a check-in before Thursday"), a pre-drafted guardian message, and a future student-facing nudge
  • Teachers retain full editorial control — every AI-generated message requires explicit send confirmation
Finding 02

Engagement-based attendance tracking

User suggestion: track attendance by engagement, not just login — a student who logs in and goes idle shouldn't be counted the same as one who's actively participating.

Proposed feature — Active Presence Score:

  • Replaces the binary present/absent model with three states: Actively Present, Passively Present, Absent
  • Calculated from weighted signals — camera use, chat messages, poll responses, reactions, screen-share interaction
  • Surfaced per student in the live class view and rolled into the weekly engagement trend chart
  • Configurable weight thresholds per class type, with a soft re-engagement alert if the score drops below threshold for 15+ minutes

Final Positioning
"Zoom, reimagined as a classroom operating system — not a meeting tool adapted for education, but a purpose-built platform where AI, analytics, and communication converge around the teacher's workflow."

The AI Teaching Assistant is really the whole point — it turns a pile of post-class data into something a teacher can act on in seconds, not days. And because risk classification does the sorting automatically, a teacher juggling 70-plus students across three classes can still find the two who actually need a check-in, without digging through spreadsheets. None of this asks Zoom to throw away what it already is. It just asks it to finally show up for the classroom the way it's shown up for the boardroom.
